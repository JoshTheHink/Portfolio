About Me

I've been passionate about both art and games for as long as I can remember—especially the intersection of how they can come together to further an artist's vision.

Growing up, my early experiences with carpentry sparked a deep appreciation for how individual details contribute to a cohesive whole. Something I now carry into all of my art as a Game Developer.

By high school, I was fully committed to pursuing a career in the industry. Since then, I have contributed to several collaborative projects through the Game Developers Club and MonRiverGames, creating visual assets and taking creative direction. Currently, I serve as the project lead and artist for Chumps Chained, a title in development with MonRiverGames.

Chumps Chained

A complete timeline of my personal devleopment and growth while making Chumps Chained

June 2024

Intial Idea

When the game started development, it was just Cadence(The other project lead) and I. Although we had a strong vision for what we wanted, the scope started out small, and the specifics were fuzzy. We were discovering all the ways the game works, and especially visually, how the game should look. Taking inspiration from cartoons like ‘Smiling Friends’ and ‘Adventure Time’, I made this first draft of the main antagonist. “The Wizard”

July 2024

Our First Prototypes

First month in, and we have a functioning prototype for the game. One where jumping would immediately break the tendons of our poor Walter and Jellybean. Who, at this time, are the beautiful pill-shaped creatures depicted to the left, with arms being separately simulated. We knew we wanted high contrast from the players to the environment, while contrasting each other enough so that players can easily keep track of who they are.

Our vision of the gameplay loop, however, intended to have friction and cause some levels of playful frustration, is meant to primarily be fair. We, and especially I, are advocates of difficult games, but see the trap in making a game unfair in order to artificially increase difficulty. Which is a trap we didn’t want to fall into.

August 2024

Sketching out Ideas

That’s why, as we worked on this initial prototype between work and getting ready to start our Junior year, we pitched it to our Professor’s Non-profit Organization, “MonRiverGames”. We got accepted and were able to enlist a programmer to help us over the summer, but more importantly. We would have a small team of students during the school year who are volunteering for class credit to help. While we still had to work on this outside of class. We always knew we wanted the chain physics to persist past the gameplay, so we created many drafts where we could implement the physics of the chain into UI elements, such as the pause screen. We envisioned this would help with charm and make the UI feel immediately readable as part of the game world and not isolated from it. Although this did take a while past August, it did work and had the intended result and more.

September 2024

Leadership Begins

As fellow students onboarded onto the project. We had to learn how to lead and coordinate a group of 11(13, including leads). 5 Computer Science students were part of a separate class that coordinated with 6 from our class of dedicated Game Design students. This meant leading the project and making sure tasks were assigned and doable. That we can put out fires, do our own programming and art, try our best to keep things on time; And the most difficult aspect, communicating with our team and making sure they communicate with each other. We had to use pictures of whiteboard sessions we had to visually communicate things like level design and mechanic design between classes. Words got too muddled at times. Which was rough at the beginning, the Computer Science students and Game Design students met at the same time but in different classrooms, and coordinating outside of class was very difficult. At times during this first semester, it felt like phantoms would make a project on the game from both classes' perspectives, thankfully mostly productive things, but still unknown phantoms. However, the biggest issue with learning how to communicate was balancing the difficulty of the game without using unnecessary frustration. It was worked on slowly during the summer, and it was mostly concept art and mechanics that didn’t work yet. Everyone new came into the project imagined something different, and it was hard to make those visions align with each other, let alone using them to enhance the original vision.

October 2024

Make or Break

This was the month that could have crushed and broken the project. I was out of commission for the entire first half of the month due to unforeseen health issues, and the communication errors kept adding up on a technical level; But we planned ahead and made some decisions prior that helped us immensely. First, the Co-Leader strategy worked as intended; a single person being out of a project shouldn’t halt progress. Secondly, we tried our best not just to lead but also to help create, so we can coordinate everything visually. Even if a lot of the art ended up being replaced soon after, the game is readable. We slowly built a library of references for artists on the team to help coordinate with each other, alongside more official guides. Even at this time, certain mechanics on a physical level were not working in the slightest and were causing issues. We at least had a coherent visual identity to keep development feeling smooth and giving us motivation to keep seeing where this game goes.

November 2024

Colors are Crazy

However, this didn’t mean the game was perfectly visual; we had to take a few steps back before more work was done and cover color theory. The colors, although bright and pretty on the characters, weren’t color blind friendly. The foreground and background muddled together, and just generally didn’t feel right. We had to make concepts for a lot of the visuals and rethink colors. We also took this approach so we could divert manpower over to fixing the chain physics. Those that were working sometimes now were extremely buggy. We discovered that with this game, we picked the one mechanic that makes programming a 2D platformer, from being the standard level of difficulty that comes with making a game, to an intimidating task. Any small change would brutalize our repository, as we had to fundamentally change how the chain worked with every change alongside the camera, so it could keep track of both players in a non-buggy fashion. This combination, along with organizing our aesthetics, made each build look and play differently every single week.

December 2024

A Clearer Vision

As our semester was winding down and we had to prepare to offboard our Game Design students, we started playtesting. We had multiple solutions and ideas for how we wanted to go forward with the chain. Our original vision was to have the arms of the players be animated and simulated separately, wildly flailing around as the players made any movement. This created A LOT of problems, and we discovered through playtesting. That it really didn’t enhance the game, players didn’t seem to notice, and those who commented on it thought it was broken. It also muddled up our visual language, and players kept getting distracted by it and complaining about its supposed broken nature. We also, after a while in development, weren’t charmed by the visual either. We didn’t get anything from it, and it was pushing back our development. So we had to scrap that idea. Although it created delays, we felt animating and programming were much simpler from this point forward. We also knew we had something special on our hands. The playtests went successfully, people would ask engaged questions, and were communicating with each other as intended. It wasn’t perfect, not even close; But it was proof that a minimal prototype worked and we knew where to head.

January 2025

Clarity of Action

The new year started with us retaining our Computer Science team but replacing our original Game Design team with 3 new people instead of retaining the original 6. We had a better idea of where the game was visually, with clear directions on where to head. More of the art from this semester was considered final and usable as opposed to the previous semester. We focused on this due to an upcoming event later that semester. GDC 2025 was on its way, and a lot of the team, including me, were extremely excited but also nervous. Our studio was going to pick one project from a list of games to show off and table; we were picked for this tabling, and the excitement and nervousness increased tenfold. We had to rush to prepare.

February 2025

Rush to GDC

The chain and character animations were quickly finalized. All of the mechanics we had during this time were working and didn’t break the chain. We had to use a triple-joint system with 3 different methods of connection checking and verification to keep the components together and prevent the camera from breaking. This allowed us to finally include our swing mechanic, where they have to balance on hanging surfaces with each other. It also allowed us to include things like pull, which can help with the swing gameplay, or be used to purposely frustrate the other player. We finally felt we had reached the simple mechanics with a wide variety of uses. Even if there were several bugs to fix, we had new clarity. This, on top of the other project lead, Cadence being out of commission for health reasons for the whole month, led to us learning a lot about crunch time and figuring out what’s needed for a certain sprint and what’s not needed.

March 2025

Steam Launch & GDC

Our UI came together in time for GDC, physically being simulated and falling when you pause the game. With our animation system being improved with a wider variety of animations that better track where the players were, and play a wider variety of animations using that context. We still had plenty of bugs, but we had fewer. So we had to use what we got and uploaded our first build onto Steam. Our initial steam launch was made in order to give those who are interested the ability to track the project. It was an avenue for us to make connections with the studio as a whole, while making the game more public during this time. GDC went phenomenally, and we learned so much from the experience, to the point that the feedback and vision were overwhelming. It would take us months and months just to incorporate what we got from watching developers play and give feedback.

April 2025

Going Forward

The implementation began as we worked on what we already have. We made plans on new levels, how we would introduce new mechanics, and how we could continue to further the visual identity. Focusing on how we can introduce new mechanics and characters that fit our established aesthetic.

May 2025

Opening Comic

As we approached the end of the semester, we looked ahead to how we could establish the story of the game better. The game visually made sense, and the gameplay made sense. Everything that was in there is coherent. But players were confused as to why. Our focus wasn’t to create an intense story; however, we had a story planned that we feel greatly enhances the game. So we drafted out the idea of an animated opening comic that shows how the story begins and establishes what the art and gameplay are going to be like by emphasizing the chain and the personalities of Walter, Jellybean, and the world they inhabit.

June 2025

Growth as a Leader

Development slowed down during the summer, but thankfully not as much as we expected. We retained 2 of the Computer Science team and 2 of our Game Design team, who are volunteering their time to work over the summer on the game. Which we considered a big victory, not just in terms of development, but showing our growth as leaders, most people who have worked on the project or are working on the project are doing it for class credit and portfolio credit. We felt this was proof that we had a game worth growing into its full potential, but more importantly, that we had grown enough as leaders and artists to adequately lead and foster a good working environment.

July 2025

Small Details

Work continued over the summer as we slowly grew the environment; a lot of supplementary details were drawn and added to the project. We redesigned some of the levels to better teach players more efficiently on how to play and use the more in-depth mechanics of the game that aren’t immediately clear.

August 2025

Launching Back into Action

At the beginning of a new school year, it was clear with our new and old team merging together, now reaching the size of 13(15 total), that we had grown a lot as artists and leaders. Our vision was coherent enough that we felt everyone was much closer to being on the same page this time around, along with being able to onboard new programmers and artists in a way that didn’t feel daunting. The style guide is very defined, and our efforts to properly document for new programmers worked out. This gave us the chance to experiment with new mechanics, such as sticky walls and pigeons that attack the players, just to see what works and what sort of gameplay loop additions would benefit the game.

September 2025

Boss Fight

Come September, we started looking far ahead. Given our new consistent pace, we planned out how we want the first boss fight to function; And how each level will connect to each other and what knowledge carries over to find a good balance and flow state. We were also able to crank out a lot of new art starting around this time that was otherwise taken up by other leadership and programming tasks.

October 2025

Preparing for 2nd GDC

On top of the development process, we started applying to the IGA conference. A sub-section of GDC where we can get a lot of visibility if we rank well in the finals of the student section. This ranking could allow us to show off at GDC again, and with a much more complete game and vision. We started to create more promotional material for the game for this submission and for future promotions. This has helped extend our style guide to seeing how the art style looks, not just in-game as assets but also as posters.

November 2025

Revisiting Clarity

This month, we have kept updating the game visually, specifically revisiting clarity and readability. The game has received a lot of art since the last time we visited readability, and the visuals have become muddled. So we have started to experiment again, trying to maintain the visuals that keep the world feeling alive while reworking them to be readable. This, on top of more solid promotional material that we can use in a wide variety of contexts.

Art Process

How I go from ideation to creation

Step 1

Ideation and Experimentation

When I start any piece of art, I start with a few references and make a very basic outline in order to feel the general layout and vibe, but very open to changes. This is to experiment with ideas of what works and to get a basic idea of what core colors the asset is going to have.

Step 2

Compare and Contrast

From here, if the asset is part of an existing project, I always import other assets and compare everything from line work to perspective in order to redo the asset. The goal of this is to take what already works from the first step and use that to make an improved outline and color scheme that fits the project as a whole.

Step 3

Revize and Finalize

Finally, I start to shade and make a finalized version. If the asset is an animation, I do like to make a completed version through to step 3 for one of the frames before I go on to step 2 and 3 for the rest of the frames. This is to help guide me with how the rest of the frames are going to look and minimize the number of times I’ll have to redo work.
